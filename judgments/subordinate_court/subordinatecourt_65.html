Public Prosecutor v Abdul Qayyum Bin Abdul Malik

Public Prosecutor v Abdul Qayyum Bin Abdul Malik
[2021] SGDC 89

Case Number : District Arrest Case No. 908524 of 2019 and Others, Magistrate's Appeal No. 9106 of 2021
Decision Date : 11 May 2021
Tribunal/Court : District Court
Coram : Christopher Goh Eng Chiang
Counsel Name(s) : Samuel Yap (Attorney-General's Chambers) for the Prosecution; Lim Ghim Siew Henry (GS Lim & Partners) for the Accused.
Parties : Public Prosecutor — Abdul Qayyum Bin Abdul Malik

Criminal Procedure and Sentencing – Sentencing

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in MA 9106/2021/01.]

11 May 2021

District Judge Christopher Goh Eng Chiang:

Introduction

1       This is an appeal against sentence by the Accused in person.

2       The Accused, who was then represented by counsel, pleaded guilty to three charges for importing drugs into Singapore under s 7 of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) (“MDA”) and punishable under s 33(1) of the MDA.

3       One other similar charge under s 7 of the MDA was taken into consideration for the purposes of sentencing.

The Facts

4       The Accused admitted to Statement of Facts (“SOF”) without qualification. A summary of the facts is set out below.

5       Sometime in November 2017, the Accused met one male Malaysian known as “Jay” in Malaysia. “Jay” instructed the Accused to make three drug deliveries into Singapore using the Accused’s own Malaysian registered white motorcar (“the Car”). The Accused agreed to do so.

6       “Jay” informed the Accused that the drugs would be concealed near the dashboard area of the Car. The Accused was to drive the Car to a location in Singapore and leave the Car unlocked for someone to retrieve the drugs. The Accused successfully completed two such deliveries for “Jay”.

7       The third delivery took place on 7 November 2017. “Jay” informed the Accused that he, ie the Accused would be delivering “ice” (street name for methamphetamine) and, ecstasy to Block 11 Chai Chee, Singapore.

8       On 7 November 2017, at about 2220 hrs, officers from the Immigration and Checkpoints Authority (“ICA”) stopped the Accused for a routine check at the Woodlands Checkpoint (“WC”). ICA officers searched the Car and retrieved five black taped bundles which were concealed within the areas behind the speedometer and the dashboard. The Accused was then placed under arrest.

9       The five black taped bundles were cut open and the contents therein were sealed in separate tamper-proof bags and submitted to Illicit Drugs Laboratory of the Health Sciences Authority (“HSA”) for analysis.

10     A total of 13 certificates dated 28 February 2018 were issued under s 16 of the MDA stating the results of the analysis. The contents of the seized exhibits contained the following amount of drugs:

(a)     not less than 18.17g of ketamine from one exhibit, the subject matter of DAC-908524-2019 (“the ketamine charge”);

(b)     not less than 2.17g of MDMA from one exhibit, the subject matter of DAC-908525-2019 (“the MDMA charge”); and

(c)     not less than 400.61g of methamphetamine from eleven exhibits, the subject matter of DAC-932997-2019 (“the methamphetamine charge”). The Accused was charged for a reduced charge of importing “not less than 249.99 grams of methamphetamine.”

11     Methamphetamine, ketamine and MDMA are Class A controlled drugs listed in the First Schedule to the MDA. The Accused was not authorised under the MDA or the Regulations made thereunder to import any controlled drug into Singapore.

12     I found the Accused guilty and convicted him on the three charges for importing the drugs into Singapore under s 7 and punishable under s 33(1) of the MDA.

Antecedents

13     The Accused has no antecedents.

Submissions on sentence

Prosecution’s submissions

14     The Prosecution sought a global sentence of 30 years’ imprisonment and 24 strokes of the cane, broken down into the following individual sentences:

(a)     Importing not less than 249.99g of methamphetamine: 25 years’ imprisonment and 15 strokes of the cane (consecutive);

(b)     Importing not less than 18.17g of ketamine: five years’ imprisonment and 5 strokes of the cane (consecutive); and

(c)     Importing not less than 2.17g of MDMA: five years’ imprisonment and 5 strokes of the cane (concurrent)

15     As the Prosecution had submitted for the prescribed mandatory minimum sentences for the ketamine and MDMA charges, its sentencing submission related to the importation of methamphetamine

16     The sentencing submission for the methamphetamine charge was based on the guidelines laid down in Vasentha d/o Joseph vs Public Prosecutor [2015] 5 SLR 122 (“Vasentha”) and endorsed by the Court of Appeal in Suventher Shanmugam v Public Prosecutor [2017] 2 SLR 115 (“Suventher”) at [28]-[31], in particular at [30] where the Court set out the indicative starting point for the unauthorised import or trafficking of cannabis:

Weight of Cannabis

Sentencing Range

330 to 380g

20 to 22 years’ imprisonment

381 to 430g

23 to 25 years’ imprisonment

431 to 500g

26 to 29 years’ imprisonment


17     The first step was to look at the weight of the drugs. The Prosecution submitted that the indicative starting point should be 29 years’ imprisonment as the amount of methamphetamine was just shy of the capital threshold of 250g.

18     The second step was to consider the Accused’s culpability. The Accused had co-operated with the authorities after his arrest. This resulted in two other persons being arrested. The Prosecution submitted that a downward adjustment to 27 years’ imprisonment would be appropriate.

19     As the ketamine and MDMA charges carried a mandatory imprisonment term of five years’ imprisonment and, that two of the three sentences of imprisonment had to run consecutively, pursuant to s 307 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, Rev Ed 2012) (“CPC”), the Prosecution proposed that, in view of the totality principle, the sentence for the methamphetamine charge should be adjusted downwards to 25 years’ imprisonment.

Mitigation plea

20     The following is a summary of the mitigating factors put forward by the Defence.

(a)     Co-operation with the authorities. Upon his arrest, the Accused cooperated with the authorities. When his car was searched, the Accused received a call from “Jay”. He was late arriving at the rendezvous point and the person, one ‘Arab” whose job was to retrieve the drugs from his car had been waiting for the Accused for a long time. The Accused did not tell “Jay” that he had been arrested. Instead, as directed by the officers present, he told “Jay” that he was held up by traffic. He maintained communication with “Jay” as he was directed by where to meet “Arab”. He also managed to obtain “Arab’s” description from “Jay”. His actions led to the arrest of two persons and he later learnt that “ … Jay’s syndicate in Singapore was eliminated… ”.[note: 1] He also informed the authorities where the drugs were hidden in the Car.

(b)     Culpability. The Accused was only a courier “in the bigger scheme of events”.

(c)     Remorse. The Accused had pleaded guilty and has vowed to turn his life around. He hoped to return home to his parents. The fact that he was originally charged with a capital offence was a frightening lesson which will remain deeply etched in his memory for the rest of his life.

21     The Defence “the minimum imprisonment sentence of 25 years’ imprisonment and 20 strokes of the cane on the charge in [the methamphetamine charge] and [the ketamine charge], and for the sentence in [the MDMA charge] to run concurrently”.[note: 2]

Sentencing Considerations

The punishment prescribed by law

22     For the methamphetamine charge, the offence attracts a mandatory minimum of 20 years’ imprisonment and 15 strokes of the cane with the maximum sentence being 30 years’ imprisonment or life and 15 strokes of the cane.

23     Both the ketamine and the MDMA charges, attract a mandatory minimum sentence of five years’ imprisonment and five strokes of the cane with the maximum sentence being 30 years’ imprisonment or life and 15 strokes of the cane per charge.

Arriving at the appropriate sentence

24     In Suventher at [30], the Court of Appeal endorsed the sentencing framework in Vasentha. This is as follows:

(a)     determining the indicative starting point based on the quantity and type of controlled drug;

(b)     make necessary upward or downward adjustments based on the offender’s culpability and any relevant aggravating of mitigating factors; and

(c)     taking into account the time spent in remand, where appropriate.

The indicative starting point

25     In Adri Anton Kalangie v Public Prosecutor [2018] 2 SLR 557 (“Adri Anton Kalangie”), the offender was charged with importing not less than 275.44g of methamphetamine. Like the Accused in the present case, the offender eventually pleaded guilty to a reduced non-capital charge of importing not less than 249.99g of methamphetamine.

26     In Adri Anton Kalangie at [80], the Court of Appeal held that “ …extrapolating an analogous framework for the trafficking or importation of 167g to 250g of methamphetamine from the Suventher framework, the indicative starting sentences should be as follows (see also Pham Duyen Quyen v PP [2017] 2 SLR 571 at [55]):

26–29


27     At [81], the Court of Appeal further held that the :

     “….sentencing framework …. in so far as the trafficking and importation of drugs are concerned …. is to be chiefly measured by the quantity of the drugs involved. Indicative starting sentences should thus be broadly proportional to the quantity of the drugs trafficked or imported. In this case, the charge against the Accused was framed for the importation of not less than 249.99g of methamphetamine. Thus, under Band 3 of the sentencing framework, the appropriate indicative starting sentence should be between 26 and 29 years’ imprisonment, or more specifically, at the higher end of this range.”

28     In the present case, the Accused was convicted for importing 249.99g of methamphetamine. The indicative starting sentence thus fell within Band 3 but on the higher end of this range. I agreed with the Prosecution that that the indicative starting sentence was 29 years’ imprisonment.

29     At this point, I note that in the Plea-in-Mitigation, Counsel had submitted for the mandatory minimum sentence of 20 years’ imprisonment to be imposed for the methamphetamine charge. However, this submission was not supported by law or by precedent. I found this submission untenable and accordingly did not put any weight on this.

Culpability of the Accused

30     The Accused had agreed to have his own vehicle used to transport the drugs. He was fully aware of what he was doing. In fact, the Accused was delivering drugs for “Jay” for the third time when he was arrested.

31     Set against this, however, I note that the Accused was a mere courier of the drugs. Hence, I was of the view that these factors did not warrant any upward or downward adjustment of the sentence.

Aggravating and mitigating factors

32     The Prosecution did not highlight any aggravating factors.

33     On the other hand, the were mitigating factors present. The Accused had cooperated with the authorities after he was arrested. While the courts have generally considered that the offender’s co-operation is not a strong mitigating factor where there is overwhelming evidence against the offender, substantial mitigating weight may be given in cases where the offender extends his co-operation beyond his own confession (see Vasentha at [73]). This was such a case.

34     After being arrested, the Accused assisted the authorities by keeping up the pretence with “Jay” that he was held up by traffic and was still on the way to the drug drop off point. He also managed to obtain the description of “Arab”, who was scheduled to pick up the drugs. This deception and information obtained by the Accused allowed officers from the Central Narcotics Bureau (“CNB”) to arrest “Arab” and another member of the syndicate.

35     While I found the Accused’s co-operation with the authorities significant, there was, however, no evidence to suggest that this led, as suggested by the Defence, to the elimination of “Jay’s” syndicate in Singapore.

36     Notwithstanding this, I adjudged that a substantial downward adjustment from the indicative starting sentence was warranted. This would be more than the two years’ imprisonment suggested by the Prosecution. Accordingly, I adjusted the sentence downwards, instead, by three years, with the indicative sentence now being 26 years’ imprisonment for the methamphetamine charge.

37     Although there were other mitigating factors submitted by the Defence, I was of the view that they did not warrant any further downward adjustment of this sentence. If it did, it would have been minimal at best. For example, I agreed with the Prosecution that, although the Accused had pleaded guilty, limited weight should be given as the Accused had been caught red-handed at WC in the car he was driving.[note: 3] As per the Chief Justice in Vasentha at [71], “Mitigating weight should only be given in deserving cases where it is clear that the admission of guilt was genuinely made out of remorse.”

38     Having determined that the appropriate sentence in this case was a sentence of 26 years’ imprisonment, I proceeded to review the sentences that have been imposed on offenders facing similar charges for the importation or trafficking of drugs just shy of the capital charge weight limit.

39     Having done so, I was of the view that the sentence of 26 years’ imprisonment was in line with the sentencing precedents.

The ketamine and MDMA charges

40     With respect to the ketamine and MDMA charges, the Prosecution had submitted for the mandatory minimum sentence of five years’ imprisonment and five strokes of the cane. The Defence agreed with this submission.

41     The quantity of drugs in these two charges was high. There was no necessity to impose a sentence higher than the mandatory sentence prescribed by law. The proposed sentence was in line with sentencing precedents. For example, for ketamine, in PP v Tan Thiam Eng [2014] SGDC and PP v Lam Tat Fei [2017] SGDC 226 the offenders were convicted after trial for trafficking of 8.59g and 20.87 g of ketamine respectively and were sentenced to the mandatory sentence prescribed by law.

The appropriate sentence: a final look

42     As the Accused faced imprisonment terms for the three charges that he pleaded guilty to, pursuant to s 307 of the CPC, the court was required to order two of those sentences to run consecutively. In the present case, this would mean a total sentence of 31 years’ imprisonment, with the sentence of imprisonment in the methamphetamine charge to run consecutively with either that imposed for the ketamine or the MDMA charge.

43     I agreed with the Prosecution that in view of the totality principle, a slight downward adjustment of the sentence for the methamphetamine charge would be justified. I therefore adjusted the sentence for the methamphetamine charge downwards to 25 years’ imprisonment.

Conclusion

44     All considered, I therefore sentenced the Accused to:

Charge

Sentence

DAC-908524-2019, the ketamine charge

5 years’ imprisonment and 5 strokes of the cane

DAC-908525-2019, the MDMA charge

5 years’ imprisonment and 5 strokes of the cane

DAC-932997-2019, the methamphetamine charge

25 years’ imprisonment and 5 strokes of the cane


45     I ordered that the sentences of imprisonment in DAC-932997-2019 and DAC-908524-2019 to run consecutively. In addition, pursuant to s 328 of the CPC, the aggregate sentence of caning imposed by the court is limited to 24 strokes.

46     Accordingly, the total sentence imposed on the Accused is 30 years’ imprisonment and 24 strokes of the cane. The date of the sentence was to commence from 9 November 2017, the date the Accused was first remanded into custody.

47     The Accused, being dissatisfied with the sentence I have imposed, now appeals against it. He is presently serving sentence.
