Principal District Judge Victor Yeo Khee Eng:

Introduction

1       The Accused, Teo Chiah Hou, 25 years old, claimed trial to a charge of insulting the modesty of a woman (“the Victim”) by exposing his penis and stroking it with his right hand, intending that such gesture be seen by her, an offence punishable under s 509 of the Penal Code (Cap. 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

2       The alleged incident took place on 24 January 2018 at about 7.00pm in a motor car bearing registration number SLE6502J, driven by the Accused and where the Victim was his front seat passenger.

3       At the material time, the Accused who was in possession of a Private Hire Licence, rented the car and moon-lighted as a Grab Car and Grab Hitch driver. The Victim had booked a Grab Hitch ride from the Accused and the alleged offence took place during the journey.

4       At the end of the trial, I was satisfied that the prosecution has proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt, and accordingly, I found the Accused guilty of the charge. He was sentenced to four (4) weeks’ imprisonment. The Accused being dissatisfied with the conviction and sentence filed an appeal against both conviction and sentence. He is currently on bail pending the hearing of the appeal.

5       These are the grounds for my decision.

The Charge

6       The proceeded charge read as follows:

     MCN 9021290 of 2018

    You, Teo Chiah Hou, are charged that you, on 24 January 2018, at about 7 p.m., in a motor car bearing registration number SLE6502J, in Singapore, did make a gesture intending to insult the modesty of a woman, one [name of victim], to wit, by exposing your penis and stroking it with your right hand, intending that such gesture be seen by the said [victim], and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 509 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

The undisputed facts

7       The facts of the case were largely undisputed between the parties. At the material time, the Accused was working as a sales engineer in Philips Singapore. He rented a car and was moon-lighting as a Grab Car and Grab Hitch driver.

8       On 24 January 2018, at about 6.00pm, the Victim booked a Grab Hitch ride from the Accused to pick her up at Circular Road to Blk 135 Cashew Road, her boyfriend’s home.

9       Sometime between 6.48pm and 7.02pm, the Accused picked the Victim up in a Nissan Latio, vehicle number SLE6502J. What transpired in the car after the Accused had picked the Victim up was the subject matter of the trial. During the journey, at about 7.05pm, the Victim sent a text message to her boyfriend (“Adam”) informing him that she was going to call the police.

10     Suffice to note at this juncture, the journey was discontinued mid-way when the Victim requested that the Accused drop her off at Royal Plaza on Scotts instead of her intended destination.

11     This took place sometime between 7.10pm to 7.13pm. It was also not disputed that after the Victim had alighted from the Accused’s car, the Accused sent her 2 text messages at about 7.15pm and 7.17pm respectively. In the text messages, the Accused apologised to the Victim and thanked her for letting him “realize his mistake”. He further apologised to the Victim for the inconvenience and said that “I’ll never do it again”.

Issues to be determined

89     At the end of the trial, the main issue to be determined by the court was a question of fact, i.e. whether the Victim’s evidence was “so unusually convincing” such that her testimony alone was sufficient for the prosecution to discharge its burden of proof.

90     In Public Prosecutor v GCK and another matter [2020] 1 SLR 486, the Court of Appeal explained that the “unusually convincing” standard is used to describe a situation where the witness’s testimony is “so convincing that the Prosecution’s case [is] proven beyond reasonable doubt, solely on the basis of the evidence”: citing Public Prosecutor v Mohammed Liton [2008] 1 SLR 601.

91     Likewise in Haliffie bin Mamat v Public Prosecutor and other appeals [2016] 5 SLR 636, the Court of Appeal explained that “a complainant’s testimony would be unusually convincing if the testimony, ‘when weighed against the overall backdrop of the available facts and circumstances, contains that ring of truth which leaves the court satisfied that no reasonable doubt exists in favour of the accused’” (citing Mohammed Liton at [39]). The relevant considerations in this regard include the witness’ demeanour, as well as the internal and external consistencies of the witness’ evidence.

Assessment of the Victim’s Evidence

92     Having had the benefit of observing the Victim gave her evidence, I found her to be a credible witness, and that her evidence contained “a ring of truth”.

93     During her testimony, the Victim gave evidence that was clear, coherent, and she could recall what happened in the car with the Accused. She was candid with her answers and did not embellish her evidence in any way so as to cast the Accused in a bad light. Her evidence describing her emotions were candid and entirely believable.

94     In my view, the Victim’s actions and behaviour after alighting from the Accused’s car were entirely consistent with her account of her having experienced something unpleasant or alarming during the ride in the Accused’s car.

95     As submitted by the prosecution, the Victim had told Adam about the incident very shortly after it happened, and thereafter, Adam also called the Accused to confront him for exposing his penis to the Victim.

96     When the Victim called Adam after alighting from the Accused’s car and recounted what happened, Adam described her as sounding very shocked and hyper, like her adrenaline was running. This was consistent with the Victim having just experienced something alarming.

97     I found the Victim’s evidence to be materially consistent with what she recounted to her boyfriend. Adam’s testimony in court and recollection of what the Victim had told him were largely consistent with the Victim’s evidence in the material aspects, in particular, what she saw the Accused did in the car, how she tried to handle the situation by striking up a conversation with the Accused, and eventually confronting the Accused about the incident.

98     Furthermore, the Victim’s evidence in court was also materially consistent with the police report that she filed a day after the incident. In the said report (exhibit P4), she recounted the details from the start of her ride and what she noticed the driver to be doing, to the time she felt something was amiss and saw that the Accused was using his hand to touch his exposed penis.

99     She also related in the police report that fearing for her safety, she started a conversation with the Accused, who then covered his penis using his hand. She finally took up the courage to confront the Accused about his actions. While the Accused initially “acted blur”, he eventually apologised and admitted that it was the first time that he committed such an act in front of his passenger.

100    The defence submitted that there were clear omissions and inconsistencies between the Victim’s evidence in court and the police report lodged by her. For example, her testimony that the Accused would “now and then” cover his penis with his hand during their conversation, whereas in the police report, the Victim stated that the Accused covered his penis using his hand upon hearing her say that they could have mutual friend as they both stayed at the Bukit Panjang area.

101    The defence further highlighted that the Victim omitted to mention in her police report that she had informed the Accused that her mother also worked at Philips Singapore, that she saw a photograph of the Accused’s fiancée on his handphone which triggered her to confront the Accused, and that the Accused allegedly offered to send her home before she asked to be dropped off at the Royal Plaza on Scotts.

102    With due respect to the learned Counsel, I did not consider these omissions to be significant. One could not expect the same level of details of conversations to be recorded in the First Information Report as compared with the testimony elicited from a witness through the rigours of the examination of witness in court. More pertinently, I observed that the Victim did not embellish her account of the incident in the police report or her testimony in court. In this respect, I could not agree with the defence’s submission that the omissions on some of the details in the police report had rendered her evidence unreliable.

103    In summary, having carefully considered the Victim’s evidence and having observed the Victim gave her evidence in court, I found that she was an unusually convincing witness, and I accepted her version of what had happened in the car. Her testimony in court was materially consistent with what she recounted to her boyfriend, and also in the police report that she filed the next day after the incident. Her reactions during and after the incident were also consistent with her being alarmed after having seen the Accused’s penis and masturbated in the car.

104    For completeness, I should state that I have considered the learned Counsel’s submission that the Victim could have been mistaken about what she had seen, such as the Accused scratching his crotch. This led to her imagination running wild and the allegation then took a life of its own.

105    However, I was satisfied that the Victim was not mistaken that the Accused was scratching his crotch as this was not supported by the evidence adduced before the court. While it was not disputed that the Accused had consulted Dr Boon on 27 December 2017 and complained of urticarial rash / hives on his upper limbs, lower limbs all over his back and some over his neck region, the evidence also revealed that he was duly treated with medication, and his condition had improved by January 2018.

106    The Accused further confirmed in his evidence during cross-examination that he did not scratch his groin or penis at any point during the journey, and that at most, he only scratched his middle inner thigh. As such, the Victim could not have been mistaken about what she saw given that she sat at the front passenger seat and would have a full view of the Accused beside her. To that extent, I also agreed with the prosecution that it would be reasonable to infer that the Accused had intended for the Victim to see his exposed penis and actions.

107    In my view, there was really no reason for the Victim to fabricate the allegations against the Accused since she did not know the Accused before the incident.

Assessment of the Accused’s Evidence

108    The accused’s defence was one of denial. He denied having exposed his penis to the Victim and that he did not know what had happened in the car that led to his passenger deciding to discontinue the journey mid-way.

109    Having carefully considered the Accused’s testimony, I did not find the Accused to be a credible witness and I found his account of the incident to be unbelievable.

110    First, I did not find the Accused’s explanations for his admission and apology to the Victim during the journey and immediately after the ride to be believable. In my view, there was no reason for the Accused to apologise to the Victim during the car ride given that the Victim had not asked to be dropped off half-way, and there was no question of him not sending his passenger to the intended destination. It was only after she confronted the Accused that she felt uncomfortable to continue the journey and decided not to proceed to her intended destination.

111    Furthermore, it was clear that the conversation between the Victim and the Accused was not about the Accused having previously dropped off a passenger halfway or mid-way of the journey, and not completing the journey but something more serious. In my view, there was no plausible explanation for the Victim to threaten to report the Accused to the police for not completing the journey, especially when it was the Victim who had wanted to discontinue the ride and declined the Accused’s offer to send her to her intended destination.

112    Secondly, I found that the two apology text messages that the Accused sent to the Victim to be the most fatal and damaging to his defence. I did not accept his explanations for sending her the two apology text messages, or the contents therein, where he admitted that he had realised his mistake, and apologised to the Victim.

113    More significantly, the Accused added in his message that he would “never do it again”. It was inconceivable and unbelievable that the Accused would be apologizing and avowing that he would never do something again, if he claimed not to know what happened in the first place. This is so if the court were to accept that the Accused was a person prone to apologise profusely for something that he did not do, either because he was working in the sales line or that he was worried about receiving a poor rating from his passenger.

114    In this respect, I found myself to be in agreement with the prosecution that the Accused’s admission and apologies merely served to confirm his guilt towards the Victim after she saw his exposed penis and what he did. Suffice to note, the apologies continued during the phone calls and text messages between the Accused and Adam. I was of the view that the Accused’s apologies could not have been for something as innocuous as dropping a passenger mid-way of the journey, but something more serious and sinister that could warrant the lodging of a police report by the passenger.

115    Thirdly, one would have reasonably expected the Accused to clarify any misunderstanding with his passenger what was it about the ride or journey that she was unhappy about, instead of admitting to a false allegation such as exposing his penis to his passenger.

116    Furthermore, I found it rather odd that the Accused would be begging Adam for forgiveness so as not to disappoint his parents and loved ones because of his “one-time mistake” of not sending his passenger to the destination, when it was abundantly clear that this was not the purpose of Adam’s call. As submitted by the learned DPP, this was more consistent with the guilt of the Accused.

117    The defence also submitted that given the clothes that the Accused was wearing at the material time, that he was driving a new vehicle that he had just collected, and that he needed both his hands to drive over a distance in the wet weather conditions, it would be practically impossible for the Accused to have committed the alleged act.

118    However, I was not persuaded by the submission as it was not the case of the prosecution that the Accused had unfastened and/or unzipped his trousers while he was driving. As submitted by the prosecution, the Accused could have exposed his penis during the early part of the drive when the Victim thought that he was fidgeting with his seatbelt.

119    Furthermore, it was also not the prosecution’s case that the Accused was touching his penis constantly throughout the drive. The Victim’s evidence was that she did not look directly at the Accused’s penis again after she had confirmed that he was masturbating. The Victim was not able to recall where the Accused’s hands were for the rest of the journey, neither was it her evidence that the Accused’s right hand was on his lap for the entire duration of the drive.

120    Finally, I was not able to agree with the learned Defence Counsel’s submission that the Victim could have fabricated the allegations against the Accused as a precursor to demand money from him and his family, or that she was inspired by the #MeToo movement against sexual harassment. There was simply no basis or any evidence to support this contention.

Verdict

121    In the final analysis, I found the Victim to be an unusually convincing witness and I found her to be a truthful witness and I accepted her testimony. Conversely, I did not accept the Accused’s explanations and his defence, and I disbelieved the Accused’s denial.

122    Accordingly, I was satisfied that the prosecution has proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt, and I found the Accused guilty of the charge.

Sentencing

Prescribed Penalty

123    The prescribed punishment for an offence under s 509 of the Penal Code is an imprisonment term which may extend to one year, or with fine, or with both.

Antecedents

124    The Accused had no antecedents.

The Prosecution’s Submission on Sentence

125    The prosecution submitted that the primary sentencing consideration in the present case was one of general deterrence. The learned DPP highlighted that the Accused committed the offence whilst in his capacity as a Grab driver performing the functions of a public transport worker. In the interests of public safety, and to deter would-be drivers to take advantage of their passengers, a deterrence sentence was called for. The prosecution submitted a sentence of at least 6 week’s imprisonment.

126    The prosecution also highlighted several aggravating factors present in this case. Specifically, the offence took place in the confined space of the taxi, which would have heightened the Victim’s state of fear. Being at the front passenger seat and within the easy reach of the Accused, she would not have been able to exit the car while it was moving.

127    The Accused’s actions were brazen, persistent and deliberate. It was not a fleeting or momentary act. The Victim saw the Accused stroking his penis twice, and that was after she initially thought he was fiddling with his seatbelt.

128    Finally, the Accused displayed a lack of remorse and he lodged a police report the day after the incident to disclaim his liability. In his police report, he lied that he had not exposed or stroked his penis in front of the Victim. He had also elected to claim trial.

129    The prosecution also cited precedent cases for the offence of indecent exposure and public masturbation, which showed a sentencing range for such offences to be between two (2) to six (6) weeks’ imprisonment.

130    In this respect, the learned DPP submitted that the facts of the present case were most similar to the case of Public Prosecutor v Cheng Tim Ann (MCN-900725-2015), where the public transport worker exposed his penis to a female passenger (21 years old), while driving his taxi. He used his left hand to continuously stroke his penis. While doing so, he repeatedly asked the victim if she saw anything, despite her reply that she did not see anything as she was afraid, even though she had noticed his exposed penis. The taxi driver, who was traced for a section 294 Penal Code offence, was sentenced to six (6) weeks’ imprisonment.

The Mitigation

131    In his mitigation plea, the learned Defence Counsel highlighted the mitigating factors that the Accused has no prior antecedents, at the time of the commission offence, he was only 23 years old, and he was of good character.

132    His learned Counsel urged the court to consider that the time the parties spent in the vehicle together was between 15 to 20 minutes. There was no physical contact and the Accused did not utter any threats against the Victim.

133    His learned Counsel further submitted that the Accused was merely giving the Victim a ride back home through Grab Hitch, and that he was not performing the function of a public transport worker as he was not operating the vehicle as a private car hire driver at the material time of the incident.

134    It was also highlighted that the offence was not premeditated and the Victim in this case was not a young child or teenager but an adult victim.

135    His learned Counsel also informed the court that the Accused has suffered adversely from the extensive media coverage about the incident, and has lost his job and had trouble finding employment.

136    The defence submitted that the sentencing considerations should be one of rehabilitation and general deterrence, and submitted that a fine of not more than $2,000 would be an appropriate punishment, citing cases where fines had been imposed for a s 509 Penal Code offence.

137    In particular, the defence relied on the case of GCO v Public Prosecutor [2019] SGHC 31 which case involved the Appellant, who had entered a female toilet when he heard someone showering, and peeped over the cubicle partition to see the victim who was showering and fully naked. The victim noticed the Appellant and immediately sought help. The Appellant remained in the shower cubicle and eventually surrendered to the security.

138    On appeal, the High Court substituted a sentence of one month’s imprisonment with a fine of $5,000. The High Court considered that there was no aggravating factor in the use of technology and held that the key consideration was whether the offence was premeditated. On the facts of that case, there was nothing to suggest that the Appellant had given a great deal of foresight or planning prior to committing the offence: GCO at [101].

139    The defence submitted that as the Accused’s actions were not premeditated and he has no antecedents, and the Victim was not a young child but an adult, a sentence of a fine not exceeding $2,000 would be appropriate.

Reasons for Sentence Imposed

140    In deciding on the appropriate sentence to impose, I was of the view that the predominant sentencing consideration in this case must be one of deterrence.

141    In Public Prosecutor v Heng Swee Weng [2010] 1 SLR 954, V K Rajah JA (as he then was), held that public transport providers, such as taxi drivers, have a “special position” vis-à-vis their passengers. All passengers travelling in taxis are entitled to expect at all times, that they can do so in complete safety without being taken advantage of by taxi drivers, regardless of whether they are fare passengers or not. Accordingly, a strong message has to be sent out to those working in the public transport service sector that behaviour taking advantage of more helpless commuters will and shall not be tolerated by the courts: at [20] and [28].

142    His Honour also referred to the case of Public Prosecutor v Neo Boon Seng [2008] 4 SLR(R) 216, where Chan Sek Keong CJ imposed a custodial sentence for a property offence against a taxi driver who had misappropriated items left behind by a passenger. CJ Chan was of the view [at 16] that “public policy would therefore call for deterrent sentences to discourage dishonesty in taxi drivers. […] the courts should send a message that any dishonest conduct will not be condoned and the benchmark punishment will be a custodial sentence, save in exceptional circumstances.”

143    While I accept that the Accused was not working as a full-time public transport provider at the material time of the incident but was moon-lighting as a Grab Car and Grab Hitch driver, I agreed with the prosecution that in the interest of public safety of the passengers, the court must send a clear message that such conduct will not be condoned.

144    The landscape of public transport in Singapore has evolved over the years with the participation of other providers of transport to the public. Private hire car services, such as Grab, have become more prevalent and are gaining acceptance as an alternative to licensed ride-hailing taxi operators. These private hire car drivers can be considered as performing a form of “public service” to our commuters, offering the public with a wider choice of transportation options, with different and more competitive fare structures.

145    Hence, as rightly submitted by the prosecution, there was no reason why these private hire cars should not be held to an equivalent standard. When a passenger booked a ride, whether from a private hire car or taxi, there would still be a legitimate expectation that the driver would be responsible for safely driving the passenger to the intended destination, and without the passenger’s modesty being outraged or insulted by the driver.

146    While I recognised that there were different schemes and booking platforms for private hire cars, such as Grab Car, Grab Share and Grab Hitch which the Accused could operate, with due respect to his learned Counsel, I was not persuaded by the submission that there ought to be a distinction between the Accused committing the offence whilst he was operating under the Grab Hitch or Grab Car mobile app platform. In my view, it would be too artificial a distinction to draw as a matter of sentencing policy.

147    The fact of the matter was that the Accused saw it fit to commit the offence against his female passenger, whom he had just picked up, barely a few minutes into the journey. The offence was committed in the confined space of a car, and the Victim who was seated beside him at the front seat, was the lone passenger. As the evidence revealed, she was evidently shocked and scared during the journey. She was afraid that the accused would turn aggressive towards her. Unlike other cases of “flashing” in a public place, the Victim could not flee or extricate herself from the situation after having started the journey, and would have been in a heightened state of alarm and fear, not knowing what else the Accused was capable of doing.

148    However, I also note that the evidence showed that when the Victim initiated conversation with the Accused, he periodically covered his penis with his hands and did not show any aggression, anger, or signs that would cause the Victim to be worried. In fact, she found sufficient courage to confront the Accused, who then became very apologetic and apologised to the Victim and said that he had realised his mistake and would never do it again.

149    While the prosecution submitted that the Accused had displayed an “utter lack of remorse”, the evidence adduced at trial also showed that the Accused had repeatedly apologised to the Victim and later, to her boyfriend and pleaded for a chance and their forgiveness in the hope to settle the matter. Unfortunately, it was when the Accused did not agree to admit to his parents and fiancée that the matter came to be reported to the police.

150    While I have considered that this was the Accused’s first offence, and that he was relatively young in age when he committed the offence, I was not able to agree with the learned Defence Counsel’s submission for a fine to be imposed. In my view, a custodial sentence was warranted in the present case to meet the objective of a general deterrence, and this clearly outweighed the rehabilitation principle, considering that the Accused had not shown any remorse for his offence notwithstanding his apologies to the Victim and boyfriend.

151    In my view, the cases cited by the defence in support of the submission of a fine could be distinguished. In the case of GCO v Public Prosecutor, which involved a “peeping Tom”, the offence was quite different in character and nature, and there were no aggravating factors, such as the use of technology to film the unsuspecting victim who was showering in the toilet cubicle.

152    As for the case of Public Prosecutor v Ali Hossain Late Abdul Hakim [2008] SGDC 380, likewise there were no aggravating factors, neither did the principle of general deterrence feature strongly in that case. The Appellant was delivering materials to the victim’s home that was undergoing renovation when he unzipped his jeans and exposed his penis to the victim. The victim was frightened and left the home immediately and took the lift downstairs, and the Appellant followed her in the lift.

153    In that case, the offensive act was for a very short duration as the victim ran out immediately. He did not further harm the victim and repeatedly apologised to the victim, who was an adult and not a vulnerable victim. In the circumstances, the court was of the view that a custodial sentence was not warranted and imposed a fine of $2,000 as he was not a “hardened criminal”.

154    As for Mr Wee’s submission that the Accused was of good character, that he has suffered through the extensive media coverage about the incident and has lost his job, and that he would have trouble finding employment, it is trite law that these are hardly relevant mitigating factors and should be given limited or no weight by the sentencing court, having regard to the key sentencing objectives. Any adverse consequences or any hardship occasioned to the Accused arising from the conviction was brought upon by his own actions. [see Stansilas Fabian Kester v Public Prosecutor [2017] SGHC 185.]

Conclusion

155    In the final analysis, I was of the view that the principle of deterrence was the primary sentencing consideration in this case, and it clearly outweighed the rehabilitative principle submitted by the defence. I was of the view that a short custodial sentence was warranted to deter like-minded persons from committing such offences.

156    In this regard, having considered the aggravating factors highlighted by the prosecution, and giving some due consideration to the mitigating factors submitted by the defence, I calibrated the sentence submitted by the prosecution downwards to four (4) weeks’ imprisonment. I was of the view that the sentence imposed was in line with the table of precedent cases tendered by the prosecution, and that the sentence would adequately meet the sentencing objectives and serve the ends of justice.

157    All considered, the Accused was sentenced to four (4) weeks’ imprisonment.

158    The Accused being dissatisfied with the decision had lodged an appeal against both his conviction and sentence. He is currently on bail pending appeal.
